On the Question of Quantum Gravity 1

Dennis Kane
August 28, 2023

Consider a finite, Gaussian-like, continuous field of scalar values2 in a Euclidean space of n-dimensions. I denote the set of radially symmetric (n-1-spherical) terminal points of the field as "the boundary". It is easy to see that applying a gradient operator to such a scalar field yields a vector field that everywhere points inwardly.

A material particle, introduced anywhere into the field, will be compelled towards the boundary3, which behavior may only be interpreted, cosmologically4, as resulting from some bizarre sort of universal law of repulsion5. Such an other-worldly physical picture only persists, though, as long as the background space has a non-positive curvature6, i.e. its geometry is either flat7 or hyperbolic8.

If, contrary to our initial assumptions, however, the background space happens to be of a constant positive curvature9, and assuming that the "reference-framed" material system fully covers10 the extent of the background space, then the boundary will necessarily become "squeezed", so to speak, into a non-singular point11. But since this purely topological transformation does not affect the direction of the gradient—i.e. the boundary-pointing nature of the physical flow is left perfectly unscathed—any newly introduced particles into this system can now easily be seen as having an attraction towards a point, rather than a repulsion away from one.

So, requiring none other than the basic notions of "Gaussian scalar fields" from the quantum theory and "positive curvature induced compactification" from topology, a mathematically robust picture of a local gravitational action12 has been attained. Such a picture perhaps gives one a nice intuition13 for how the phenomenon of gravity in our own universe might really14 work.

Secondary Footnotes

a. Tim Maudlin has made the point that, in stark contrast to the received wisdom of introductory level chemistry and physics students everywhere, the creation of an exact, ontologically-grounded definition—or even a loose-but-well-reasoned signification—for those perfect theoretical abstractions, nicely rendered in said students' textbooks, called "atomic orbitals", is far from having been attained. In the Bohr picture, they were understood to be the effect of planet-like electrons that revolved, classically, about a star-like nucleus. But then de Broglie envisioned them as somehow being diffused throughout space in a standing-wave kind of pattern, which is the thread that Schrödinger tugged in order to imaginatively deduce the concept of vibratory wave functions. By this point in time (ca. mid-1920's), fuzzy intuitions still had some leeway to probe for "mechanically proper" schemes for a wholly rational—even perhaps a priori calculable—theory of light and matter, although such "vision-think" would've been so exceedingly speculative as to possess almost no conceivable mortal purpose. So, Born—by this time something of an elder statesman to a community of unwashed upstarts, though still, himself, Einstein's junior—then developed his namesake "rule" to interpret the now-smeared-out "orbitals" as fields of probabilities related to the outcomes of [probably "national government prioritized"] scattering-type experiments. Mathematically, though, "what atomic orbitals are" needn't have any more metaphysical significance than the quite Platonic notion of being a rather voluminous breed of sinusoidal curve. Physicists, of course, must have a hard time swallowing hypotheses to the tune of, "Our entire material world results from nothing other than systems of undulating higher-order-trigonometry-type objects, each with their peculiar forms, scales, frequencies and phase alignments". Contra the physicists, though, one might argue that they perhaps should dig a little deeper into Leibniz's oeuvre for the purpose of gaining a better sense of his highly "quixotic" (read as: fervently anti-Newtonian) Weltanschauung...

b. This motion should be seen to arise by virtue of a fundamental application of standard particle-flowing-in-a-field type of thinking. One should not think that there is any reason—at the current level of [purely mathematical] construction—to mandate any determinate species of physical field, because the entire point of this exercise is to gain some sense of a kind of, as Kant would have it, "unknowable noumenal reality" that underlies all possible appearances. Perhaps this form of reasoning should be termed "dynamical mathematics" as opposed to any kind of physics: mathematical, theoretical, or otherwise. Along these lines, we might want to assert that mathematics consists of the totality of intuitively discernable concepts that must first of all be intrinsically believed before any of the knowledge-based sciences (starting with physics) can rightfully start getting underway. Such thinking might lead us to understand that one must first be capable of "believing things" in order for "knowing things" to have any proper meaning.
