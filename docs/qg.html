On the Question of Quantum Gravity 1

Dennis Kane
August 28, 2023

Consider a finite, Gaussian-like, continuous field of scalar values2 in a Euclidean space of n-dimensions. I denote the set of radially symmetric (n-1-spherical) terminal points of the field as "the boundary". It is easy to see that taking the gradient of such a scalar field yields a vector field that everywhere points inwardly.

A material particle, introduced anywhere into the field, will be compelled towards the boundary3, which behavior may only be interpreted, cosmologically4, as resulting from some bizarre sort of universal law of repulsion5. Such an other-worldly physical picture only persists, though, as long as the background space has a non-positive curvature6, i.e. its geometry is either flat7 or hyperbolic8.

If, contrary to our initial assumptions, however, the background space happens to be of a constant positive curvature9, and assuming that the "reference-framed" material system fully covers10 the extent of the background space, then the boundary will necessarily become "squeezed", so to speak, into a non-singular point11. But since this purely topological transformation does not affect the direction of the gradient—i.e. the boundary-pointing nature of the physical flow is left perfectly unscathed—any newly introduced particles into this system can now easily be seen as having an attraction towards a point, rather than a repulsion away from one.

So, requiring none other than the basic notions of "Gaussian scalar fields" from the quantum theory and "positive curvature induced compactification" from topology, a mathematically robust picture of a local gravitational action12 has been attained. Such a picture perhaps gives one a nice intuition13 for how the phenomenon of gravity in our own universe might really14 work.

Secondary Footnotes

a. As Tim Maudlin points out—in stark contrast to the received wisdom of undergraduate chemistry students everywhere—the creation of an exact, ontologically-grounded definition (or even a loose-but-well-reasoned signification) for those perfect abstractions called "atomic orbitals" is far from having occurred. In the Bohr picture, they were understood to be the work of planet-like electrons that revolved (classically) about a star-like nucleus. But de Broglie then envisioned them as somehow being diffused throughout space, which is the thread that Schrödinger tugged in order to deduce the concept of vibratory wave functions. At this point in time, fuzzy intuitions could still be invoked in order to investigate "mechanically proper" schemes for an a priori calculable theory of light and matter, but this would've been so exceedingly speculative as to possess almost no conceivable mortal purpose. So, Born (by this time something of an elder statesman to a community of unruly upstarts, though still, himself, Einstein's junior) then developed his namesake "rule" to interpret the waves as fields of probabilities related to the outcomes of the politically en vogue regime of scattering-type experiments. Mathematically, though, "what atomic orbitals are" needn't have any more metaphysical significance than [the highly Platonic notion of] being a rather voluminous breed of sinusoidal curve. Physicists, of course, must have a hard time swallowing the thesis that our entire material world results from nothing other than interactions between undulating objects of a kind of higher-order trigonometry, each of said objects with their peculiar forms, sizes, frequencies and phase rotations. Perhaps if physicists only pried a little deeper into Leibniz's oeuvre in order to gain a better sense for his highly "quixotic" (read as: fervently anti-Newtonian) Weltanschauung, they might think twice about rejecting it so out-of-hand!

b. This motion should be seen to arise by virtue of a fundamental application of standard particle-flowing-in-a-field type of thinking. One should not think that there is any reason—at the current level of [purely mathematical] construction—to mandate any determinate species of physical field, because the entire point of this exercise is to gain some sense of a kind of, as Kant would have it, "unknowable noumenal reality" that underlies all possible appearances. Perhaps this form of reasoning should be termed "dynamical mathematics" as opposed to any kind of physics: mathematical, theoretical, or otherwise. Along these lines, we might want to assert that mathematics consists of the totality of intuitively discernable concepts that must first of all be intrinsically believed before any of the knowledge-based sciences (starting with physics) can begin to get underway. So this might lead us to think that we must first be capable of believing things in order for knowing things to have any proper meaning.
